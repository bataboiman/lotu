A Bible Study for PNG Churches

The Unhewn Altar

Lotu Tru — True Worship. Why God asked for uncut stones, and what that means for our churches today.

In many churches today, we work very hard to make worship sound good and look good. Big speakers. Skilled musicians. Powerful preaching voices. None of these things are evil. But the Bible gives us a strong warning: God never asked us to make worship impressive. He asked us to make it true. This short study walks through six Bible passages that all say the same thing, and then asks what they mean for our churches in Papua New Guinea.

Lotu tru i kam long bel. True worship comes from the heart.

1. The Stone God Refused to Let Us Carve

In the Torah, God gave Israel a surprising rule about building His altar. "Hewn" means cut and shaped with a tool. God said: do not do that.

"And if thou wilt make me an altar of stone, thou shalt not build it of hewn stone: for if thou lift up thy tool upon it, thou hast polluted it."

Exodus 20:25

"Thou shalt build the altar of the LORD thy God of stones: thou shalt not lift up any iron tool upon them."

Deuteronomy 27:5

Think about that. The moment a chisel touched the stone, the altar was polluted — made unclean. Not improved. Not beautified. Polluted.

Why? Because God wanted the altar to show His provision, not human skill. If the stones were carved and polished, people would look at the altar and say, "What a fine builder!" God wanted them to look at the altar and see only Him. The beauty was in the untouched stone that God Himself made.

2. The Praise of Babies

"Out of the mouth of babes and sucklings hast thou ordained strength because of thine enemies, that thou mightest still the enemy and the avenger."

Psalm 8:2

A baby has no theology degree. No music training. No stage presence. No strong voice. A baby simply comes as it is — small, weak, and fully dependent on someone bigger.

And God says that this is the praise that silences His enemy. Not the polished song. Not the perfect performance. The raw, unpolished cry of someone who knows they need God for everything.

This is the unhewn altar again, in human form. God is not looking for what we can add. He is looking for hearts that come empty-handed.

Kam long God olsem liklik pikinini. Come to God like a small child.

3. Worship in Spirit and Truth

When Jesus spoke to the woman at the well, she asked Him about the right place to worship — this mountain or Jerusalem? Jesus moved the whole question somewhere else:

"But the hour cometh, and now is, when the true worshippers shall worship the Father in spirit and in truth: for the Father seeketh such to worship him. God is a Spirit: and they that worship him must worship him in spirit and in truth."

John 4:23–24

Worship is no longer about the right building, the right decorations, or the right performance. It is an inward reality. "In spirit" means the Holy Spirit moves it, deep inside a person. "In truth" means the life honestly lines up with God — no acting, no show.

Notice the Father is seeking such worshippers. He is not seeking better sound systems. He is seeking hearts.

4. Paul Threw Away the Chisel

Paul was highly educated. He could have preached with clever, beautiful speech that would impress any crowd. He chose not to — on purpose:

"And my speech and my preaching was not with enticing words of man's wisdom, but in demonstration of the Spirit and of power: That your faith should not stand in the wisdom of men, but in the power of God."

1 Corinthians 2:4–5

Paul saw the danger: if people believed because his words were impressive, their faith would stand on him. He refused to carve the altar with the chisel of clever speech, so that faith would rest on God's power alone.

The prophet Zechariah heard the same message centuries earlier:

"...Not by might, nor by power, but by my spirit, saith the LORD of hosts."

Zechariah 4:6

Not by human strength. Not by human skill. By God's Spirit alone.

Spirit bilong God tasol inap. Only God's Spirit is enough.

5. The Danger Today: When Feelings Drive the Lotu

Here is where we must be careful and honest with ourselves.

Many services today are built to create a feeling. The music swells. The lights change. The preacher raises his voice at just the right moment. People cry, jump, and shout — and we say, "God moved today!" But did He? Or did the production move us?

The quiet lie

The danger is this: we slowly teach people to believe that if it sounds good and looks good, God accepts it. But God does not judge worship by pitch, volume, lights, or crowd size. He judges the surrendered heart. Excitement made by equipment is not the same as the moving of the Holy Spirit.

Be careful here — this is the part people get wrong in both directions. The Bible does not say skill is sin. It does not say emotion is sin. David played skilfully. The psalms are full of loud joy and deep tears. The problem is not skill or emotion. The problem is reliance — leaning on them.

When the Spirit leads and a heart truly worships, emotion often follows — tears, joy, awe. That is good fruit. But when we use music and hype as fuel to manufacture the emotion, and then call that feeling "worship", we have picked up the chisel. We are carving the stones.

Modern hewn stones

What does the chisel look like today?

  • Musical talent as the offering. Skilled music sounds beautiful. But if the worship leader is relying on vocal technique instead of a surrendered heart, it is a man-made offering.
  • Eloquence as the anointing. Fine speech and stage presence can make a sermon exciting to hear. But if the preacher relies on speaking skill instead of prayer and the Spirit, the words never pierce the heart.
  • Production as proof. When a church measures a service by how professional it looked and sounded, the focus has shifted from a broken, humble heart to a spectacle.

Here is a simple test. If the power went off — no speakers, no lights, no instruments — would the worship still stand? If yes, the equipment was only a servant. If no, the equipment had become the altar.

God i no skelim naispela nois. Em i skelim bel. God does not measure the nice sound. He measures the heart.

6. A Practical Guide for Our Churches in PNG

This message is good news for Papua New Guinea. Think of a small village haus lotu — no electricity, no sound system, no instruments except hands and voices. By the world's measure, it has nothing. By the Bible's measure, it may be an altar of perfect uncut stones. Nothing man-made is in the way.

And for our town and city churches with full bands and speakers — the gear is not the enemy. It simply asks for more watchfulness, the way Paul watched his own eloquence.

For worship leaders and musicians

  • Prepare your heart before you prepare your instrument. Pray before you practise.
  • Choose simple songs the whole congregation can sing — worship belongs to everyone, not only to those on stage.
  • Practise well, then lean on God, not on the practice. Skill is a servant, never the offering itself.
  • Ask: would I still lead worship with the same joy if there were no microphone and no stage?

For preachers

  • Depend on prayer and the Word, not on stage tricks or a loud voice used only for effect.
  • Say it simply. Paul chose plain words so that faith would stand on God's power, not on man's wisdom.
  • Aim for hearts pierced by the Spirit, not ears entertained by delivery.

For the whole congregation

Worship does not end when the service ends. Romans 12:1 says:

"I beseech you therefore, brethren, by the mercies of God, that ye present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy, acceptable unto God, which is your reasonable service."

Romans 12:1

The Greek word for "reasonable" is logikēn (λογικὴν) — spiritual, rational service. Worship is a daily, surrendered life, not a theatrical display. Your worship continues on Monday: in the garden, at the market, in the office, on the PMV. An honest day's work, a clean tongue, a forgiving heart — that is the living sacrifice.

Givim laip bilong yu olgeta long God — long Sabat, Sande na long olgeta de tu. Give your whole life to God — on Sabbath, Sunday and every day.

Askim yu yet — Ask yourself

  • If the sound system failed today, would my worship still stand?
  • Am I coming to God like a baby — empty-handed and dependent — or like a performer?
  • Do I judge a service by how it made me feel, or by whether hearts surrendered to God?
  • When I sing or preach, am I relying on my skill, or on the Holy Spirit?
  • Does my worship continue on Monday — in the garden, the market, the office?
  • Is there anything in our church that shines a light on human talent instead of on God?

The Beauty of the Uncut Stone

God's rule about the altar was never about stones. It was about us. He was teaching His people, from the very beginning, that He does not want our polish. He wants our hearts — rough, honest, and fully His.

So come as you are. Sing with the voice you have. Pray with the words you know. Let skill serve quietly and let emotion follow freely — but let the Holy Spirit alone carry the worship. That altar, built of uncut stones, is the one God calls clean.

May the Lord of hosts, who works not by might nor by power but by His Spirit, find in you a true worshipper — in spirit and in truth. Amen.
